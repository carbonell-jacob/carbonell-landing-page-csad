Why Software Engineering Isn’t Just About Coding

By Jacob S. Carbonell · February 3, 2026 · 4 min read

Credit: Unsplash

Will I just code?

Many believe that software engineering is only a process of coding in front of a computer screen. But that is not really the case. I believe that it takes more than just being great in programming to be a good software engineer. It’s not always about looking for solutions to complex problems or finding what technologies are best fit for the job, because most of the time, you will be put in situations that feel outside your comfort zone. Therefore, to thrive in this field, you need to be familiar with your creative or interpersonal skills.


Personally, I always ask myself if I am fit to become a software developer in the future. It’s a dream of mine to work and collaborate with other people who have the same goals as I do, but sometimes I question if I really have what it takes to do it. I thought that I just needed to be good at coding, but I was wrong. When I saw that even the best get laid off or couldn’t get a job, I realized that even if I have great technical skills, it won’t guarantee success, there is more to it than that. I felt discouraged and a little hopeless at first, but I knew that there are things that I should do to work on myself as an aspiring developer.


Soft Skills in Software Engineering

A developer’s technical skills are important, because it serves as the foundation for everything else in the tech field. Without it, why even bother in becoming a software developer? But as I said earlier, it isn’t the determining factor of success. Developers not only code, they also interact with others. After researching the process of how software is developed, I was surprised to see that the implementation or coding phase is just the tip of the iceberg. Underneath it involves the usage of my interpersonal and creative skills for the designing of the software. During these processes I must communicate with other developers and designers well. Communication is important because you will collaborate with other people with the goal to ensure that all of you meet the requirements and standards of your client or boss.


Before writing code, software developers must understand user needs first. This means that they must identify what problem should be solved in the first place. This will involve me communicating with others to identify what requirements must be met, and turning them into clear solutions. I realized that this isn’t as straightforward as I thought it would be because given the time constraint, I must also make trade-offs to balance between time and quality. It is important to know when to say “no” during these situations because the more features you implement, the more the software is prone to failure. The skills that I need during these processes will involve critical thinking and empathy, not just technical knowledge.


Even during the implementation phase, software is rarely built alone. Developers must work with teams, review each other’s code, and explain technical concepts to non-technical people in a way that is easy to understand. This made me realize that to be a great software developer, you must value teamwork and lower your pride. Values that affect your relationship with others are just as important as intelligence. At the same time, I must consider that the tech industry constantly evolves which means that I must also be willing to learn and adapt to new technologies. I learned that success in software engineering depends more on persistence and one’s willingness to grow than on knowing everything from the start.


What now?

Software engineering is not just about coding, but about solving problems in general. Writing code is just the tip of the iceberg, it is only one part of what makes a developer successful. The ability to communicate effectively, work well with others in a team, thinking creatively in designing, and adapting to constant change plays an equal role. I realized that the doubts and fear that I feel is an unavoidable part of my growth, not signs of failure, because it helps me in determining what I must do to improve on myself continuously, rather than being ignorant, building fake confidence that stops growth. Software developers always adjust, they never stop learning, and they continuously condition themselves to adapt to new environments. As an aspiring software developer, I now see that it’s not all about the knowledge that I possess, but the character I continue to build.
